Worldbuilding: Middle-earth Geography

Key locations and their thematic significance. Each place in Middle-earth has a feeling that should come through in the writing.

The Shire

English countryside. Green rolling hills, round doors, gardens, ale. Represents innocence, the world worth saving. Must feel real and specific — not generic pastoral. Tolkien modeled it on rural Warwickshire.

Rivendell

The Last Homely House. A place of wisdom, rest, and old beauty. Autumn feeling — golden, fading, a little melancholy. The Elves know their time is ending.

Moria

Dwarven grandeur gone to ruin. The horror of something ancient and forgotten waking up. Vertical spaces — the shafts, the bridge, the endless stair. Claustrophobia alternating with vertigo.

Rohan

Anglo-Saxon horse culture. Open grasslands, golden halls, strong simple people. Theoden's court at Edoras on its hill. The feeling of a culture under siege from within (Wormtongue) and without (Saruman).

Mordor

Industrial wasteland. The anti-Shire. Nothing grows, nothing lives, the air itself is poisonous. Tolkien drew on his WWI experience — the blasted landscapes of the Somme. Must feel physically oppressive in the prose.

Minas Tirith

Seven-tiered city carved from white stone. Beautiful but besieged. The White Tree dead in the courtyard — the kingdom waiting for its king. Think: Constantinople meets Mont Saint-Michel.
